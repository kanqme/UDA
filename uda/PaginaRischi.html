Understanding the Risks of E-Waste

Electronic waste contains numerous hazardous components that, when improperly disposed of, can release toxic substances into the environment. These substances pose a serious threat to human health, both for those who work directly with electronic waste and for communities living near inadequate landfills and recycling centers.

On this page, we explore in detail the main health risks associated with e-waste and the ways these harmful substances enter our bodies and the environment.

Heavy Metals in E-Waste

Lead

Pb

Sources in E-Waste:

  • Cathode ray tubes (CRT) in old televisions and monitors
  • Soldering on printed circuit boards
  • Lead-acid batteries

Health Effects:

  • Damage to the central and peripheral nervous system
  • Kidney and circulatory system damage
  • Delays in cognitive development in children
  • Problems with coordination and concentration
  • Potential increase in blood pressure

Mercury

Hg

Sources in E-Waste:

  • Switches and relays
  • Fluorescent lamps and backlighting
  • Batteries
  • Thermostats

Health Effects:

  • Damage to the brain and nervous system
  • Impairment of neurological functions
  • Kidney problems
  • Vision and hearing disorders
  • Impacts on the digestive and immune systems
  • Particularly harmful to fetal development

Cadmio

Cd

Sources in E-Waste:

  • Rechargeable batteries (Ni-Cd)
  • Anti-corrosion coatings
  • PVC stabilizers
  • Old cathode ray tubes

Health Effects:

  • Classified as carcinogenic to humans
  • Damage to kidneys and skeletal system
  • Respiratory problems and lung disorders
  • Interference with calcium metabolism
  • Possible damage to the reproductive system

Other Dangerous Chemical Substances

Brominated Flame Retardants (BFR)

Sources in E-Waste:

  • Plastic casings of electronic equipment
  • Printed circuit boards
  • Cables and connectors

Health Effects:

  • Interference with the endocrine system
  • Potential effects on neurological development
  • Possible carcinogenic effects
  • Can accumulate in fatty tissues and breast milk

Polychlorinated Biphenyls (PCBs)

Sources in E-Waste:

  • Capacitors and transformers
  • Old electrical equipment
  • Cooling fluids

Health Effects:

  • Liver damage
  • Effects on the immune system
  • Developmental problems in children
  • Potential carcinogen
  • Endocrine disorders

Case Studies: Impact of E-Waste on Communities

Guiyu, China

Once considered the largest e-waste dump in the world, Guiyu has suffered from severe environmental and health problems:

  • Blood lead levels in children significantly higher than average
  • Contamination of groundwater and agricultural soils
  • Increased cases of respiratory and skin problems
  • Higher rates of spontaneous abortions among women of reproductive age

Agbogbloshie, Ghana

One of the largest e-waste disposal sites in Africa, where workers, including many children, burn cables to recover copper:

  • Direct exposure to toxic fumes during the burning of materials
  • High levels of heavy metals in workers' blood and urine samples
  • Contamination of surface water and soil
  • Chronic respiratory problems and skin injuries among workers
