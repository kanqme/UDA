Understanding E-Waste

E-waste, short for "electronic waste," refers to all electrical and electronic devices, their parts, and consumables that are discarded without the intention of being reused. These include computers, smartphones, tablets, household appliances, televisions, and many other devices we use daily.

With technological advancement and the shortening of the lifecycle of electronic products, the amount of e-waste generated globally is continuously increasing, creating one of the most significant environmental challenges of our time.

Dangerous Components In E-Waste

Electronic devices contain numerous toxic substances that, if not disposed of properly, can cause serious environmental and human health damage:

Heavy Metals

  • Lead: Found in cathode ray tubes (CRTs) and solder on printed circuit boards
  • Mercury: Found in switches, relays, and fluorescent lamps used for backlighting
  • Cadmium: Present in rechargeable batteries and coatings to prevent corrosion
  • Hexavalent Chromium: Used as an anti-corrosive agent

Other Dangerous Substance

  • Brominated Flame Retardants (BFR): Used in plastic casings
  • PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride): Common in cables and casings
  • Beryllium: Found in connectors and springs
  • Phosphorus: Used in cathode ray tubes

Global Statistics on E-Waste

  • Every year, more than 50 million tons of electronic waste are generated worldwide
  • Only 20% of e-waste is properly documented and recycled
  • 80% of e-waste ends up in landfills or is managed informally, often in developing countries
  • It is estimated that by 2030, global e-waste production will reach 74 million tons per year
  • The value of raw materials in global e-waste is estimated at over 62 billion dollars annually

Environmental Impact

When e-waste is improperly disposed of, toxic substances can seep into the soil and groundwater, contaminating ecosystems and water reserves. Additionally, the open burning of e-waste, a common practice at informal disposal sites, releases toxic pollutants into the air.

Responsible recycling of e-waste not only prevents pollution but also allows for the recovery of valuable materials, reducing the need to extract new limited resources.
